# Epilepsy

## Overview
Epilepsy is a neurological disorder characterized by recurrent, unprovoked seizures. A person is diagnosed with epilepsy if they have two unprovoked seizures more than 24 hours apart, or one unprovoked seizure with a high risk of recurrence.

## Types of Seizures
**Focal (Partial) Seizures:**
- Focal aware (simple partial): Consciousness preserved; motor, sensory, or autonomic symptoms
- Focal impaired awareness (complex partial): Altered consciousness; automatisms (lip smacking, hand rubbing)
- Focal to bilateral tonic-clonic: Starts focal, spreads to both hemispheres

**Generalized Seizures:**
- **Tonic-clonic (Grand Mal)**: Loss of consciousness, stiffening (tonic), then rhythmic jerking (clonic)
- **Absence (Petit Mal)**: Brief staring spells; common in children; 5–30 seconds
- **Myoclonic**: Brief, sudden muscle jerks
- **Atonic (Drop attacks)**: Sudden loss of muscle tone; falls
- **Tonic**: Stiffening of muscles
- **Clonic**: Rhythmic jerking movements

## Causes
- Genetic factors (idiopathic epilepsy)
- Brain injury (trauma, stroke, tumor)
- Infections (meningitis, encephalitis)
- Developmental disorders (autism, neurofibromatosis)
- Metabolic disorders
- Unknown (cryptogenic) — 50% of cases

## Diagnosis
- **EEG (Electroencephalogram)**: Records brain electrical activity; shows epileptiform discharges
- **MRI brain**: Identifies structural causes (tumors, malformations, scars)
- **Blood tests**: Glucose, electrolytes, CBC, metabolic panel
- **Video-EEG monitoring**: Gold standard for seizure classification

## Treatment
**Anti-Seizure Medications (ASMs):**
- First-line: Levetiracetam, lamotrigine, valproate, carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine
- 70% of patients achieve seizure control with medication
- Treatment usually continued for 2+ years after last seizure

**Drug-Resistant Epilepsy (30% of patients):**
- Epilepsy surgery (resection of seizure focus)
- Vagus nerve stimulation (VNS)
- Responsive neurostimulation (RNS)
- Deep brain stimulation (DBS)
- Ketogenic diet (high-fat, low-carbohydrate)

## First Aid for Seizures
- Stay calm; time the seizure
- Protect from injury; cushion head
- Turn person on their side (recovery position)
- Do NOT restrain; do NOT put anything in mouth
- Call emergency services if: seizure >5 minutes, no recovery, injury, first seizure

## Lifestyle Considerations
- Avoid seizure triggers (sleep deprivation, alcohol, stress, flashing lights)
- Driving restrictions (varies by country; typically seizure-free for 6–12 months)
- Swimming and bathing safety precautions
- Medication adherence is critical
